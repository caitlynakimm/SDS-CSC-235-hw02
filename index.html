Exploratory Data Analysis: EMS Incident Dispatch Data in New York (2025)

Synopsis

Link to dataset.

This dataset contains information on over 1 million incidents reported and stored in the EMS Computer Aided Dispatch System for New York in 2025. It serves to understand the allocation of resources and the Fire Department's response to emergencies. The dataset is provided by the Fire Department of New York City annually and was last updated October 29, 2025.

Data Overview

Each row of the dataset represents one incident created and closed in the dispatch system. It contains details such as the date the incident was created and closed, initial and final call types and severity levels, the dispatch response and travel times, and the location of the incident (by borough and districts). The incident response time is the time from the incident being recorded to the time first on scene. The incident travel time is the time from the first unit assignment to the time first on scene.

Biases in the Data

Bias present in the data could come from how the EMS employees' interpreted incident calls or perceived the actual incidents. Due to the high stress work environment and personal biases towards certain demographics of people, call service workers may have underestimated incident severity levels or misclassified call types and on-scene workers may have underestimated their travel times for a better performance outlook or forgot to reassess severity levels and call types.

Visualizations

Summary Statistics

Quantitative Variables: Incident Response Time and Incident Travel Time

The mean, median, first quartile, and third quartile (in minutes) for both the response time and travel time are shown. The median response time is 7.88 minutes and the median travel time is 7.38 minutes. So, the process of recording an incident and assigning a unit may have taken about 30 seconds. Both the mean and median response times are greater than the same measures for travel time. This makes sense since response time covers the entire duration of an incident. The interquartile range of response time is 5.11 minutes and 5.1 minutes for travel time, so the spread of the middle 50% of both times are about the same.

Ordinal Variables: Initial Severity Level and Final Severity Level

The initial severity level ranges from 1 (least serious) to 9 (most serious). The most common initial severity level reported is 5 (249,364 reports).

The final severity level ranges from 1 (least serious) to 8 (most serious). Interestingly, level 9 is omitted from the range of levels. The most common final severity level reported is also 5 (251,617 reports).

Categorical Variables: Final Call Types and Boroughs

The top 10 most common final call types for incidents are shown. The most common type is sickness (122,501 reports).

New York is made up of the 5 boroughs shown. There is one incident where its borough location was not reported. The borough with the most incidents reported is Brooklyn (285,754 reports).

Relationships Between Variables

This series of histograms display the distribution of travel times for each borough in New York 2025. All 5 boroughs show a left-skewed distribution and generally show most travel times took between 5 to 7.5 minutes.

This paired bar chart compares the frequency of severity levels for initial and final type reporting in New York 2025. For levels 1, 3, 5, and 6, there are slightly more incidents with the final severity type.

This stacked bar chart compares the top 10 final call types by borough in New York 2025. The most common final call type in the Bronx is sickness, the most common in Brooklyn is injury, the most common in Manhattan is sickness, the most common in Queens is sickness/injury, and the most common in Richmond/Staten Island is injury.
